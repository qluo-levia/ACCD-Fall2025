What Do Prototypes Prototype?

“We propose a change in the language used to talk about prototypes — to focus on what they prototype rather than on what they are made of.”

This statement is insightful; it fundamentally transforms design thinking. Designers are no longer bound by the superficial aspects of tools or fidelity, but instead return to the "thinking function" of prototypes. It reminds us that prototypes are not results, but a form of inquiry—a way of thinking in uncertainty.


"A prototype is any representation of a design idea, regardless of medium."

This statement broadens the definition of a prototype and challenges the traditional prejudice that "a prototype must be a digital or functional model." From storyboards to building blocks, from animations to the simulated interactive "Wizard of Oz," these can all become tools for design exploration. It makes "prototype" more of a way of thinking than a production standard.


"Prototypes themselves are not self-explanatory; it is the designer's responsibility to clarify what is and not be explored."

Audiences often misunderstand the meaning of prototypes. Designers must not only "create prototypes," but also "explain prototypes"—explaining their assumptions, boundaries, and goals. This makes prototypes not merely experimental tools, but also part of the design narrative.
